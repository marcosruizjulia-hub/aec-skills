# Method Cards

Use these cards when a diagnostic answer needs more detail. Select only the relevant cards; do not dump all cards into an answer.

## 1. Triad gate

**Move:** Evaluate stability, usefulness, and delight/legibility together.

**Ask:** What fails first: durability, use, or legibility?

**Modern translation:** Structure/envelope performance, operational fit, and design coherence must all be checked before endorsement.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What has to remain sound over time?
- What use or operational pattern must the proposal serve?
- Does the visual/formal choice make the system clearer or merely more decorative?

**Red flag:** A proposal is defended mainly by appearance, prestige, symbolism, or precedent.

## 2. Site-before-form

**Move:** Start with place: exposure, moisture, sun, wind, water, ground, access, and hazards.

**Ask:** What does this place do to bodies, materials, and operations over time?

**Modern translation:** Climate and site risk assessment before design freeze.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What are the strongest exposures by orientation?
- How does water arrive, move, collect, and leave?
- What conditions will accelerate decay, discomfort, or maintenance?

**Red flag:** A scheme ignores drainage, humidity, prevailing wind, heat gain, flooding, contamination, or maintenance access.

## 3. Use-specific distribution

**Move:** Fit layout and hierarchy to users, routines, public/private role, resources, and daily operations.

**Ask:** Who uses it, for what, and under what constraints?

**Modern translation:** Program validation, stakeholder mapping, access/circulation review.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Which users need privacy, prominence, speed, service access, quiet, shade, daylight, or security?
- Does circulation express the real sequence of use?
- Are service/back-of-house flows separated where needed?

**Red flag:** A prestigious or generic layout is applied to a different social, operational, or budget condition.

## 4. Local means discipline

**Move:** Choose what can actually be sourced, transported, built, replaced, and maintained.

**Ask:** Is the specified solution locally feasible, or is there a lower-risk substitute?

**Modern translation:** Supply-chain, budget, installer skill, embodied logistics, maintenance, and warranty review.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What is locally available and proven under similar exposure?
- What specialist labor, tool, or warranty is required?
- What happens when a component must be replaced in five or ten years?

**Red flag:** Rare materials or specialist details are chosen without performance justification.

## 5. Material sample test

**Move:** Test materials directly before relying on them.

**Ask:** What simple test would reveal weakness, contamination, incompatibility, freshness, porosity, staining, or weathering?

**Modern translation:** Mockups, coupons, pull tests, water absorption, abrasion, adhesion, salt exposure, UV exposure, corrosion checks, and substrate compatibility.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Which performance claim is unproven?
- What test would expose the failure cheaply?
- Does the test match the actual exposure and assembly, not just the isolated material?

**Red flag:** Quality is assumed from a material category, supplier claim, or visual sample alone.

## 6. Environmental adaptation

**Move:** Orient rooms, openings, services, finishes, and assemblies according to sun, wind, humidity, and thermal change.

**Ask:** What should face sun, shade, wind, stable light, or protected exposure?

**Modern translation:** Passive design, daylighting, thermal comfort, facade exposure zoning, condensation review.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Which elevations deserve different assemblies or fixing strategies?
- Where will heat, glare, rain, freeze-thaw, or condensation dominate?
- Does a uniform facade ignore non-uniform exposure?

**Red flag:** The same envelope, opening ratio, or finish is used everywhere despite different environmental loads.

## 7. Water/source verification

**Move:** Verify source quality, capacity, and route before distribution.

**Ask:** Is the source clean, reliable, protected, legally usable, and appropriate for its intended use?

**Modern translation:** Water testing, flow testing, contamination review, seasonal reliability, source protection, pressure, treatment, and commissioning.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What is the intended use: potable, irrigation, process, fire, greywater, rainwater?
- Has quality been tested by a qualified lab where required?
- Is flow reliable under dry season, peak load, and failure conditions?

**Red flag:** Pipes, storage, or distribution are designed before the source is proven.

## 8. Hidden hazard probe

**Move:** Test invisible risks before entering, loading, sealing, or commissioning.

**Ask:** What invisible condition could injure people, damage work, or invalidate the decision?

**Modern translation:** Confined-space gas tests, moisture probes, destructive openings, pressure tests, smoke tests, thermal scans, utility scans, structural investigations.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Which risk would not be visible from the surface?
- What must be tested before access or closure?
- What evidence would prove the space, cavity, or system is safe enough to proceed?

**Red flag:** Work proceeds in concealed, confined, buried, pressurized, or hazardous conditions based on confidence alone.

## 9. Commissioning by failure points

**Move:** Add access and inspection where faults are likely.

**Ask:** Where would this system fail, and how would we find or isolate the fault without destroying everything?

**Modern translation:** Access panels, isolation valves, cleanouts, drainage inspection, test ports, pressure relief, monitoring, replaceable modules.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Where does water, air, pressure, force, heat, or movement concentrate?
- Which joints, bends, supports, penetrations, transitions, or controls are most failure-prone?
- Can the failed part be inspected and replaced?

**Red flag:** A continuous hidden system has no intermediate inspection, isolation, or access points.

## 10. Module/coherence test

**Move:** Derive parts from a controlling unit and check part-to-whole relationships.

**Ask:** What module governs the system, and where does it break?

**Modern translation:** Grid, bay, panel, structural rhythm, facade module, proportion, tolerance stack, component interface, design-system coherence.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What is the unit: bay, panel, structural span, sheet size, room module, column spacing, joint rhythm?
- Where do dimensions drift or become ad hoc?
- Does a decorative element belong to the same technical and functional logic?

**Red flag:** Components meet awkwardly, dimensions drift, or borrowed motifs ignore the system they are attached to.

## 11. Substitution discipline

**Move:** When replacing an ideal material, detail, site, or configuration, name the tradeoff.

**Ask:** What performance is lost, what risk is added, and what evidence would make the substitute acceptable?

**Modern translation:** Value engineering review that compares durability, fire, moisture, warranty, installation, appearance, maintenance, carbon, and cost.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Which requirement drove the original choice?
- Which property of the substitute is weaker?
- What compensating detail, test, warranty, or maintenance plan is needed?

**Red flag:** A substitution is justified only by price or superficial similarity.

## 12. Mechanics by centers and ratios

**Move:** Analyze mechanical action through centers, leverage, rotation, ratios, counterweights, support, and restraint.

**Ask:** Where is the center, what is the arm, and how does the ratio transform effort into motion or load?

**Modern translation:** Load path, torque, gear ratio, pulley advantage, lifting system review, support reaction, braking, stability, and failure mode analysis.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What is the load and where is its center of gravity?
- What lever arm, wheel radius, gear ratio, pulley count, or counterweight is doing the work?
- What stops uncontrolled motion?

**Red flag:** A machine is described by desired effect without explaining force transmission, support, braking, or overload behavior.
